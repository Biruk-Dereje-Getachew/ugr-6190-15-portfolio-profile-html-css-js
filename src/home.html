Projects

Conway's Game of Life is a zero-player game that simulates the evolution of a grid of cells based on simple rules. Each cell can be either alive or dead, and its state changes from one generation to the next based on the number of live neighbors it has. The rules of the game determine whether a cell survives, dies, or becomes alive in the next generation. The game can produce a wide range of patterns, including stable structures, oscillators, and patterns that grow indefinitely. Despite its simplicity, the Game of Life can model complex behaviors and phenomena, making it a popular example in cellular automata and computational theory.

The Lorenz attractor is a set of chaotic solutions to the Lorenz system of differential equations, which model atmospheric convection. It is a famous example of deterministic chaos, where small changes in initial conditions lead to vastly different outcomes. The attractor is characterized by its butterfly-shaped structure, representing the unpredictable behavior of the system over time. Despite its chaotic nature, the Lorenz attractor is deterministic, meaning that the system's future behavior is entirely dependent on its starting state. It has been widely studied in chaos theory and is a striking visual representation of how complex systems can evolve unpredictably.

Fractal trees are self-replicating structures that exhibit self-similarity at various scales, meaning their smaller branches resemble the entire tree. These trees are generated using recursive algorithms, where each branch splits into smaller branches at a constant angle, creating complex, tree-like shapes. As with other fractals, fractal trees display infinite detail as you zoom in, and their structure is often influenced by mathematical rules like the angle of branching and the reduction in branch size. Fractal trees are a fascinating example of how simple, recursive patterns can create visually complex and natural-looking structures, making them a popular topic in mathematics, computer graphics, and nature simulations.
